Double The Better

16mm film is a format that has been widely used for professional, educational, and independent filmmaking. Introduced as a more affordable alternative to 35mm and with better quality than the already existing 8mm, it offered a compact size while maintaining respectable image quality. 16mm became popular in documentaries, television, some hollywood productions and student and independent films, providing a balance of portability and cinematic quality that was not possible with 8mm Film.

Style and Characteristics

16mm film has a distinct texture and grain that gives it a recognizable look. Compared to 35mm, it has slightly lower resolution but remains highly organic, Specially in artistic films that use their limitations as a strength. Its portability and affordability making it a favorite between filmmakers either independent or in the industry.

Standard 16mm Film

Standard 16mm film runs vertically with a single or double perforation per frame. It is the classic format used since its release. Despite its smaller size, standard 16mm provides sharp images suitable for many professional applications. Some films that used 16mm are: El Mariachi, The Hurt Locker, The Texas Chainsaw Massacre and The Wrestler.

Ultra 16mm

Ultra 16mm is a variation of standard 16mm with a wider image area achieved by reducing the space of the perforations. This allows for a wider aspect ratio (commonly 1.85:1) without using anamorphic lenses, giving filmmakers a more cinematic look while maintaining the low cost of 16mm. Cinematographer Frank G. DeMarco is credited with inventing Ultra 16 in 1996 while shooting tests for Darren Aronofsky's Pi.
